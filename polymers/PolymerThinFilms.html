Homopolymer Thin Films

We want to get an overview of the effects of surface energy and entropy on the morphology of a polymer thin film.
First we will look at a homopolymer, inspired by a study by van der Lee [van der Lee], and then see which
of these considereations play a role for block copolymer films.

[van der Lee et al., Langmuir 17, 7664 (2003)]

A polymer film differs from the infinitely extended bulk in that it has two interfaces: the air-film-interface and the film-substrate interface. Let's start with the first interface:

The air-film interface is dominated by the surface energy or surface tension of the polymer. The surface energy is the amount of energy needed to bring a polymer chain to the surface. Since coordination and entanglement are always reduced for a polymer chain at the surface, there is always energy needed and never gained to get the chain to the surface. In this respect the surface acts like a repulsive wall. However, the air-film interface is not rigid like a real wall, and the film surface does not have to be flat, as for instance in dewetting processes. Finally the air-film interface is the connection of the film with the outside world, and in particular diffusion processes of solvents may induce a kinetic structuring due to concentration gradients in the surface layers.

The film-substrate interface can be thought of as a hard wall, which may be attractive or repulsive. There are many subtle effects that are of importance in the interface region of the polymer material close to the wall:

Attractive Wall
- increase of Tg
- increase of monomer density at wall

Wall entropy
- chain-wall interaction: surface adhesion
- chain conformation: distorted coil shape (oblate) -> increased segment density
- loss of configurational entropy: decrease in monomer density, depletion region
- chain orientation: orient parallel to reduce orientational entropy
- surface segregation of stiffer segments

Repulsive Wall
- lowering of Tg
- disentanglement
- confinement
